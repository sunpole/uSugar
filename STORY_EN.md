# The Magomedov Family Story and the Making of uSugarBot
# 🧬 uSugar
### Your sugar, your rules
### Твой сахар под контролем
*Family diabetes assistant for Libre2 · Семейный помощник при диабете*

**Minsk, Belarus. 2026.**

There are five of us: me, Anton (40), my wife Olga (turning 42 this summer), our eldest son Nikita (turned 13 in April), daughter Vera (will be 6 in August), and our youngest son Matvey (will be 9 in September).

Nikita was diagnosed with type 1 diabetes when he was one and a half years old — mid‑2014. He was our first child. We tried to give him everything we could, even if things were far from perfect. One day he caught a severe cold, stopped eating and drinking, and had a fever above 39.5°C for days. We called the doctor to our home. She said it was just a common flu. By the third call, she refused to come: “Four days of illness — he should be fine by now, walk him to the polyclinic.” But Nikita could no longer walk. He looked like he had lost 40% of his body weight, he began falling, and on the fourth day he couldn’t move at all — he just drank water and opened his mouth.

That morning I went to work. Olga stayed with Nikita. She had been crying all night and all morning, saying it was something terrible, something about sugar. I didn’t know what diabetes was back then. Around 11 a.m. on the fourth day, Nikita started to die. When the doctor finally arrived, he was losing consciousness. An ambulance rushed him to the intensive care unit. They put him on a ventilator and fought for his life. My wife called me around 2 p.m., telling me our son was in critical condition and the doctors were almost helpless. I didn’t fully grasp what that meant, though I knew what a ventilator was — in my youth, after sports injuries to my spine, neck, and head, doctors had told me I’d never walk again, but I did. So I didn’t know how to react. A couple of hours later, I saw a photo of my almost lifeless son, covered in wires and sensors.

By evening, his condition stabilised. Only the next morning did the doctors diagnose type 1 diabetes. Nikita spent four or five days in the ICU, until he could breathe on his own. Then he was transferred to a children’s endocrinology ward. There, everything became both scarier and clearer. He improved day by day. I rarely visited. As a father, I couldn’t forgive myself for not protecting my child. My life just stopped. By then I hadn’t drunk or smoked for three years and was actively training — I couldn’t understand why this was happening.

A month later, I went to pick up my family from the hospital. Nikita had changed completely. In one month, he seemed to have regressed half a year in his physical development. The illness wouldn’t let him grow normally for the next three years — he simply sat in a wheelchair. Another month later, a medical check‑up revealed I had hepatitis C — a consequence of my past with drugs. From 16 to 25, I was a heroin and hard‑drug addict. My son’s illness and my own diagnosis shattered me. I went on a binge for two and a half years. I drank in the morning, at lunch, and in the evening — I poured myself into oblivion. I didn’t want to live. I saw no point in continuing such a failed life. I only dreamed that my son would become a normal child, but every month he seemed to get worse. My relationship with my wife almost broke. We barely spoke. I understood I couldn’t be the support they needed, and that killed me even more. I tried as hard as I could, but it was probably only a tenth of what I should have done and what was expected of me.

More than ten years have passed since then. Nikita is now thirteen. Life has changed dramatically. I found solace, and that solace saved our family, our marriage, and brought us two more wonderful children — Vera and Matvey.

Now I want to invest my free time and modest programming knowledge into building a Telegram bot that can help my son. The bot will recognise Libre2 screenshots, calculate insulin doses, keep a diary, and send reminders. Perhaps this project will be a small compensation for everything I couldn’t give during the hardest years.

**This bot is my attempt to do something that makes life a little easier for Nikita — and maybe it will inspire other parents facing the same struggle.**
